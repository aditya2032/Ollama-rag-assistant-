Cats are small domesticated carnivorous mammals scientifically known as Felis catus.
Domestic cats are descended mainly from the African wildcat, Felis lybica.
Cats were likely domesticated in the Near East thousands of years ago near early farming communities.
Cats sleep for about 12 to 16 hours each day, although some cats sleep more.
Kittens and older cats usually sleep more than healthy adult cats.
Cats conserve energy by sleeping because their bodies are adapted for short bursts of hunting activity.
A group of cats is called a clowder.
A male cat is often called a tom or tomcat.
An unspayed female cat is often called a queen.
A young cat is called a kitten.
Cats are obligate carnivores, meaning they require nutrients naturally found in animal tissue.
Taurine is an essential nutrient for cats and is important for their heart, eyes, and overall health.
Cats need a balanced commercial cat food or a properly formulated veterinary-approved diet.
Fresh, clean water should always be available to cats.
Many cats prefer drinking from wide bowls or moving water sources such as pet fountains.
Cats use their whiskers to judge whether they can fit through narrow spaces.
Whiskers are highly sensitive hairs connected to nerves and should not be trimmed.
Cats have more than 20 muscles that help control ear movement.
Cats can rotate their ears to locate sounds.
Cats can hear very high-pitched sounds that humans cannot hear.
Cats have a strong sense of smell and use scent to learn about their environment.
Cats communicate with people and other cats through body language, vocalizations, and scent marking.
Cats may rub against people or objects to leave scent from glands on their face and body.
Scratching is a normal cat behavior that helps maintain claws and leaves visual and scent marks.
Providing a sturdy scratching post can help protect furniture and support normal cat behavior.
Cats generally prefer scratching surfaces that are stable and tall enough for a full stretch.
An adult cat usually has 30 permanent teeth.
Kittens have temporary baby teeth that are later replaced by adult teeth.
Regular dental care can help reduce dental disease in cats.
The pattern on a cat's nose is unique, similar to a human fingerprint.
Most cats have five toes on each front paw and four toes on each back paw.
Some cats have extra toes, a condition called polydactyly.
Cats walk on their toes, which is called digitigrade locomotion.
Cats use their tails for balance and communication.
A raised tail with a relaxed curve often indicates a friendly greeting.
A tail held low or tucked close to the body can indicate fear or discomfort.
A cat may swish or thump its tail when it is irritated, excited, or concentrating.
Purring can be associated with contentment, but cats may also purr when stressed, in pain, or seeking comfort.
Meowing is used more often by adult cats when communicating with humans than with other adult cats.
Cats may chirp or chatter when watching birds or other prey.
Hissing and growling are warning signals that a cat wants more distance.
Slow blinking is often interpreted as a relaxed, non-threatening social signal between cats and people.
Cats use grooming to keep their coat clean and to regulate body temperature.
Excessive grooming can sometimes be a sign of stress, skin irritation, parasites, or pain.
Hairballs can form when loose hair swallowed during grooming collects in a cat's stomach.
Regular brushing can reduce loose fur and may help reduce hairballs.
Long-haired cats generally need more frequent brushing than short-haired cats.
Cats have a rough tongue because it is covered with tiny backward-facing structures called papillae.
Cats use their rough tongues to groom fur and remove loose hair.
Cats have a flexible spine that helps them twist and land during jumps.
Cats have powerful hind-leg muscles that help them jump and sprint.
Cats often prefer elevated resting places because height can make them feel safer.
Providing shelves, cat trees, or safe window perches can enrich an indoor cat's environment.
Indoor cats benefit from regular play that imitates hunting behaviors such as stalking, chasing, and pouncing.
Interactive toys should be moved in a prey-like way and stored safely after supervised play.
Laser-pointer play should end with a toy or treat that the cat can physically catch.
Puzzle feeders can provide mental stimulation and slow down eating.
Cats need a quiet, safe place where they can rest without being disturbed.
Cats often prefer predictable daily routines for feeding, play, and rest.
Sudden changes in behavior, appetite, litter-box habits, or energy level can be reasons to contact a veterinarian.
Cats commonly hide when they feel frightened, sick, or overwhelmed.
A new cat should be introduced to a home gradually, beginning with a separate safe room.
Introductions between cats should be slow and supervised to reduce stress and conflict.
For multiple cats, a common recommendation is one litter box per cat plus one extra box.
Litter boxes should be placed in quiet, accessible locations away from food and water bowls.
Many cats prefer unscented litter and a clean litter box.
Scooping litter boxes daily helps keep them acceptable to cats and makes health changes easier to notice.
Declawing removes the last bone of each toe and can cause pain and behavior problems.
Regular nail trimming and appropriate scratching surfaces are humane alternatives to declawing.
Cats should be kept indoors or given safe, supervised outdoor access to reduce risks from traffic, disease, and predators.
An outdoor enclosure, often called a catio, can provide safer outdoor enrichment.
Cats can be trained using positive reinforcement, such as treats, praise, or play.
Punishment can make cats fearful and may worsen unwanted behavior.
Clicker training can be used to teach cats simple behaviors and make veterinary care easier.
Carrier training can reduce stress during travel and veterinary visits.
Most adult cats should have regular veterinary checkups, even when they appear healthy.
Vaccination needs vary according to a cat's age, lifestyle, location, and health status.
Spaying or neutering can prevent unwanted litters and may provide health and behavioral benefits.
Microchipping is a permanent form of identification that can help reunite lost cats with their families.
An identification collar with a breakaway safety feature can also help identify a cat.
Fleas, ticks, and intestinal parasites can affect cats, so parasite prevention should be discussed with a veterinarian.
Never give a cat human medication unless a veterinarian specifically instructs you to do so.
Some common human foods, including onions, garlic, chocolate, alcohol, grapes, raisins, and xylitol, can be dangerous to cats.
Lilies can be extremely toxic to cats, and even small exposures may be dangerous.
If a cat may have eaten a toxic substance, contact a veterinarian or animal poison service immediately.
Cats should not be allowed to chew electrical cords, strings, ribbons, or small objects because they can cause serious injuries.
String-like objects can be especially dangerous if swallowed and should be kept out of reach.
Kittens need socialization to gentle people, handling, sounds, and environments during early development.
Kittens usually need more frequent meals than adult cats because they are growing quickly.
Senior cats may need dietary, dental, mobility, and health monitoring adjustments.
Arthritis can make it harder for older cats to jump, groom, or enter high-sided litter boxes.
Low-entry litter boxes and accessible resting spaces can help cats with reduced mobility.
Obesity can increase the risk of health problems and reduce a cat's quality of life.
Portion control, measured feeding, and daily play can help maintain a healthy cat weight.
Weight-loss plans for cats should be guided by a veterinarian because rapid weight loss can be dangerous.
Cats are usually most active around dawn and dusk, a pattern called crepuscular activity.
Cats may bring toys to people as part of play or hunting-like behavior.
Félicette, a French cat, became the first cat in space in 1963.
The ancient Egyptians associated cats with protection and several deities.
Black-cat coat color does not determine a cat's personality.
Coat patterns include solid, tabby, tortoiseshell, calico, colorpoint, and bicolor.
Tabby refers to a coat pattern rather than a specific breed.
Calico cats are usually female because of the genetics involved in orange and black coat colors.
Cats can have blue, green, yellow, copper, or mixed eye colors.
Many kittens are born with blue eyes that may change color as they grow.
Cats have a third eyelid called the nictitating membrane that helps protect the eye.
Healthy cats may nap frequently but should still show normal interest in food, water, and their environment.
Each cat has an individual personality, so preferred play, handling, food, and resting habits can differ.
